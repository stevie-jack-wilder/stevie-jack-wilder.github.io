What is the difference between Margin, Border, and Padding?




Let's start off with one of my essentials: A visual! What you're looking at is called The Box Model, and it is a great CSS tool to design the layout of webpages. In essense, it allows you to control empty space on a website by making changes to any or all of three things: The margin, the border, and the padding.


These three components sit all snug around your content. Content is an umbrella word you can use to refer to any visual and interactive elements on you page. This could be text, images, videos and more!


Margin is our outer-most part of the box model. It is a transparent feature that dictates the amount of space between different content on your page. You can think of Margins as magnets with only a positive charge (positve repels). The bigger the margin number, the stronger your magnetic field becomes, pushing away other content surrounding it.


Border is our most snug and sandwiched part of the box model. Unlike Margin and Padding, you can choose whether it is a transparent, colored, or designed feature. Ooh-la-la! Have a look up at the top left of this page, for example. Do you see the the border around the "Home" image in the circle? That is a border I added in css.



We can see that I created a border of 5px. Because I have only written the value once, it applies it to all sides of the image. "Wait! But there aren't four sides, Stevie!" I hear you proclaim. I know, I know. But there's this nifty little feature I added on the line below: Border-radius. Border-radius with a high pixel value has rounded out the border, and my square image now sits inside. Gorgeous.


Padding is last step inside the box model, and is a transparent feature like the margin. This time, however, instead of reacting in relation to other independent elements on your page, it does so in relation to the content and the border. Text looking a little tight around the edges? No worries! Chuck in some padding.
